\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
Chest radiography is one of the most frequently used examinations in emergency and inpatient care. In the Emergency Department (ED), chest X-rays play an important role in evaluating patients with respiratory symptoms, chest pain, trauma, and diseases related to the heart and lungs. In clinical practice, however, chest X-rays are rarely interpreted in isolation. Instead, physicians often consider them alongside other sources of clinical information, such as patient history, vital signs, and clinical reports, in order to form a more comprehensive understanding of a patient's condition.

Despite their widespread use, chest X-ray images can be difficult to interpret. Radiographic findings may be subtle, abnormalities may be obscured by overlapping anatomical structures, and image quality may vary due to differences in patient positioning or acquisition conditions. These challenges have motivated the use of deep learning methods for automated chest X-ray analysis. While such methods have shown promising results, many existing approaches rely primarily on image data alone. This limits their ability to reflect the broader clinical context that clinicians normally use during decision-making.

This thesis is primarily based on the CaMCheX framework, which investigates multimodal chest X-ray interpretation by combining radiographic images with clinical text and structured patient information. Rather than proposing an entirely new clinical prediction task, this work builds on the motivation and general direction of CaMCheX and adapts it to a more resource-constrained experimental setting. In particular, this thesis focuses on implementing and evaluating a multimodal chest X-ray classification pipeline using image features, text embeddings, and numerical vital-sign features.

The main contributions of this thesis are as follows. First, it provides an implementation and experimental study of a CaMCheX-inspired multimodal model for chest X-ray interpretation. Second, it explores practical design choices required under limited computational resources, including reduced image resolution, frozen or precomputed text embeddings, and lightweight visual backbones. Third, it investigates model interpretability through Grad-CAM analysis, allowing the visual attention patterns of the model to be examined in relation to clinically relevant image regions.

\subsection{Research Challenges}
\label{subsec:intro_challenges}

Describe the key challenges that current systems face and that this thesis aims to address:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Single-Modality Limitations}: Standard classification models only look at CXRs, missing vital clinical context.
    \item \textbf{Noisy Text Translation}: Existing fusion models translate numeric vitals into natural language, introducing translation noise and relying on imperfect text encoders.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Prior Ignorance}: Diagnostic workflows depend heavily on comparison with previous (prior) exams, yet most models analyze current studies in isolation.
    \item \textbf{Computational Bottlenecks}: Multi-modal token fusion (e.g., in dense self-attention) scales quadratically, making it expensive for real-time clinical deployment.
    \item \textbf{Severe Class Imbalance}: Medical findings exhibit highly skewed distributions, where a few pathologies dominate and many critical conditions are rare.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Proposed Solution: CaMCheX}
\label{subsec:intro_proposed}

Introduce the Clinically-aligned Multi-modal Chest X-ray Classification framework (\textbf{CaMCheX}). Briefly explain its evolution:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CaMCheX V2}: Spatially expanded fusion that aligns text and vitals features across the spatial grid of the X-ray images.
    \item \textbf{CaMCheX V3}: Slimmed single-token fusion which collapses non-image modalities to single tokens to improve computational efficiency.
    \item \textbf{CaMCheX V4}: Asymmetric prior cross-attention that structures temporal modeling as a query-decoder task, preventing prior-label copying and halving attention complexity.
\end{itemize}
Explain how the model integrates current inputs with historical records (vitals, prior labels, prior reports) while preventing label leakage.

\subsection{Thesis Contributions}
\label{subsec:intro_contributions}

Summarize the main contributions of this work:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Development of a unified multimodal architecture combining CXRs, clinical text, and numeric vitals.
    \item Design of an asymmetric cross-attention mechanism (V4) to perform compute-efficient and leakage-safe temporal comparison with prior studies.
    \item Systematic analysis of token layouts (V2 vs. V3) evaluating the trade-offs of spatial expansion in multi-modal fusion.
    \item Integration of Asymmetric Loss (ASL) with frequency-based weighting to counter extreme label imbalance.
    \item Comprehensive evaluation on a subset of the MIMIC-CXR and MIMIC-IV datasets.
\end{enumerate}
