
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Chest radiography is a fundamental imaging modality in clinical practice, particularly in emergency departments (ED) and inpatient wards where diagnostic decisions must be made rapidly. It is widely used to detect thoracic conditions such as pneumonia, pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema, pleural effusion, and cardiomegaly \cite{irmici2023cxr_emergency_ai}. However, automated chest X-ray interpretation remains challenging due to overlapping anatomical structures, subtle visual signs, and variations in image quality \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr}.

In clinical settings, chest radiographs are not interpreted in isolation. Clinicians interpret images alongside clinical context, including indications, active symptoms, vital signs, and historical studies. While deep learning has significantly improved automated chest X-ray classification, most existing models rely solely on image inputs \cite{irvin2019chexpert}. This image-only focus creates a gap between automated systems and clinical workflow, where patient history is essential for accurate diagnosis.

Furthermore, chest X-ray classification is naturally a multi-label, long-tailed problem. Multiple findings often co-occur, and many critical conditions occur far less frequently than common ones, as highlighted by the CXR-LT benchmarks \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2025cxrlt2024}. Inspired by the CaMCheX framework \cite{sloan2025camchex}, this thesis investigates multimodal chest X-ray classification under practical computational constraints. The proposed model fuses radiographic images with clinical indication text, vital signs, and prior-study context to develop a classification system that is more reflective of real diagnostic workflows.

\subsection{Background and Motivation}
\label{subsec:intro_background}

Chest X-ray interpretation is a complex diagnostic task where visual findings must be contextualized using a patient's overall clinical state. In practice, the significance of a radiographic finding often depends on non-image details such as the reason for the examination, active symptoms, vital signs, and historical imaging. For instance, a pulmonary opacity may represent acute pneumonia in a patient presenting with fever and cough, but indicate chronic atelectasis or scarring in an asymptomatic patient.

Because clinicians interpret radiographs together with indication text, history, vital signs, and prior imaging, this thesis studies multimodal classification rather than image-only prediction. While deep learning has achieved high diagnostic performance on large datasets like MIMIC-CXR and CheXpert \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,irvin2019chexpert}, conventional models remain image-centric. This limits their effectiveness in emergency and inpatient settings where clinical context strongly influences diagnostic urgency. Consequently, this thesis develops and evaluates a multimodal approach that integrates radiographs, clinical text, vital signs, and prior-study context under practical computational constraints, building upon the CaMCheX framework \cite{sloan2025camchex}.

\subsection{Task and Dataset Context}
\label{subsec:intro_task_dataset}

This thesis addresses long-tailed, multi-label chest X-ray classification. The objective is to predict the presence or absence of multiple thoracic findings from a chest X-ray study. Medical diagnosis is naturally multi-label, as patients frequently present with concurrent conditions (e.g., cardiomegaly, pleural effusion, and pulmonary oedema).

The experimental setup is aligned with the CXR-LT benchmark, which reflects clinical distributions where common conditions appear frequently while rare, critical findings occur infrequently \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2025cxrlt2024}. This severe imbalance biases standard models toward frequent labels, necessitating strategies that handle long-tailed class distributions.

Data is sourced from MIMIC-CXR and MIMIC-IV \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,johnson2023mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. These datasets are integrated to compile study images, clinical indication texts, structured vital signs, and historical prior studies.

\subsection{Clinical and Technical Challenges}
\label{subsec:intro_challenges}

Several clinical and technical challenges must be considered when developing a multimodal chest X-ray classification model:

First, image-only models have limited access to clinical context. Although radiographs contain essential visual evidence, clinical interpretation depends on indications, symptoms, and vital signs to guide diagnostic decisions.

Second, the input modalities are heterogeneous. Reconciling high-dimensional images, free-text indications, and structured numerical vitals requires encoding different formats, feature scales, and missingness patterns into a shared representation.

Third, the classification problem is long-tailed and multi-label. Severe class imbalance and label co-occurrence bias models toward common findings, requiring specialized objectives to maintain sensitivity on rare, critical conditions.

Fourth, prior-study information introduces shortcut and leakage risks. While comparing previous examinations is clinically valuable, historical reports can lead to shortcut learning if the model relies on prior text instead of analyzing the current radiograph.

Finally, multimodal fusion is computationally expensive. High-resolution images generate large token sequences, causing quadratic attention costs in transformer-based architectures \cite{vaswani2017attention} that must be optimized for resource-constrained training.

\subsection{Approach Overview}
\label{subsec:intro_approach}

This thesis adapts and extends the CaMCheX framework for multimodal chest X-ray classification under practical computational constraints \cite{sloan2025camchex}. The proposed model combines current radiographic images, clinical indication text, structured vital signs, and prior-study context into a single classification pipeline.

Each modality is first encoded using dedicated feature extraction modules: a lightweight visual backbone for radiographs, a pre-trained clinical language model for indication text, projection layers for vital signs, and temporal embeddings for prior-study information. These encoded features are then combined using a transformer-based fusion module. Rather than relying on computationally expensive dense token spaces, this thesis explores compact token layouts and feature routing strategies to balance spatial detail with training efficiency. The prior-study context is integrated as auxiliary temporal evidence to support comparative diagnosis without introducing shortcut learning.

\subsection{Thesis Contributions}
\label{subsec:intro_contributions}

The primary contributions of this thesis are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The implementation of a CaMCheX-inspired multimodal chest X-ray classification framework combining radiographs, clinical indication text, structured vital signs, and prior-study context.
    \item An investigation of practical design choices under limited computational resources, including reduced image resolutions, frozen text encoders, and compact token layouts.
    \item A prior-aware temporal modelling component incorporating historical study information and time-gap embeddings, designed to mitigate shortcut learning and leakage risks.
    \item Evaluation of loss-function choices tailored to long-tailed multi-label chest X-ray classification.
    \item Qualitative interpretability analysis using Grad-CAM visualizations \cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} to inspect visual attention maps against clinical findings.
\end{itemize}

Overall, this thesis provides an implementation, adaptation, and analysis of clinically motivated multimodal chest X-ray classification under computational constraints, rather than proposing a new clinical AI framework.
