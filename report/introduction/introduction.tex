\section{Introduction}\label{sec}

\subsection{Background and Motivation}\label{subsec}

Chest radiography is one of the most widely used imaging studies in clinical practice. Approximately two billion chest radiographic studies are performed globally each year, reflecting its role as a fast, low-cost, and widely available first-line examination for cardiopulmonary disease \cite{abbott2025challenging_cxr}. In the broader workload of medical imaging with ionizing radiation, UNSCEAR (United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation) estimates around 4.2 billion medical radiological examinations and procedures per year, including about 2.6 billion conventional radiography examinations, 1.1 billion dental radiography examinations, and 400 million CT (Computed Tomography) examinations \cite{unscear2021medical_exposure}. A typical adult chest X-ray has an effective dose of about 0.1 mSv, which is much lower than chest CT at about 6.1 mSv, low-dose lung cancer screening CT at about 1.5 mSv, and whole-body PET (Positron Emission Tomography)/CT at about 22.7 mSv \cite{radiologyinfo2025radiation_dose}. These facts explain why chest X-ray interpretation is an important clinical task: it is common, low-dose, widely available, and often used when decisions must be made quickly.

Automated chest X-ray interpretation remains challenging because thoracic findings can be subtle, overlapping anatomy can obscure abnormalities, and image quality varies across acquisition settings \cite{irmici2023cxr_emergency_ai}. Beside image quality, the task is difficult because each study may contain multiple findings and the labels are strongly imbalanced. In clinical imaging, this matters because rare or low-prevalence findings are still clinically important: missing an uncommon condition such as pneumoperitoneum or pneumomediastinum can have serious consequences, even if such findings appear far less often than common abnormalities. This reflects a broader issue in medical diagnosis, where rare conditions are collectively important but individually underrepresented in training data \cite{nguengang2020rare_diseases}. The CXR-LT-2023 and CXR-LT-2024 challenges was designed to study this problem in chest radiography, with more than 350,000 chest X-rays labelled with 26 clinical findings following a long-tailed distribution \cite{holste2024cxrlt}. In the full CXR-LT source label distribution, Support Devices appears 126,776 times, while Pneumoperitoneum appears only 813 times, creating a more than 150-fold frequency gap. This imbalance motivates imbalance-aware training and separate head, medium, and tail evaluation, as later shown in Figure~\ref{fig:logscale_label_distribution}.

In clinical practice, chest radiographs are usually interpreted together with clinical context, including the indication or history, symptoms, vital signs, and prior examinations \cite{acr2025communication,jacenkow2022indication,sloan2025camchex}. However, many widely used chest X-ray classification baselines are image-centric and use radiographs as the primary input \cite{irvin2019chexpert,johnson2019mimiccxr}. This creates a gap between automated chest X-ray classification and the clinical workflow. This thesis studies whether clinical context and prior-study information improve long-tailed chest X-ray classification. We build on CaMCheX (Clinically-aligned Multi-modal Chest X-ray Classification) work \cite{sloan2025camchex} and evaluate the integration of these modalities for chest X-ray classification.

\subsection{Task and Dataset Context}\label{subsec}

Our work studies multi-label chest X-ray classification under a long-tailed label distribution. For each chest X-ray study, the model predicts the presence or absence of multiple thoracic findings. This setting matches clinical diagnosis, where patients may have several concurrent findings, such as cardiomegaly, pleural effusion, and pulmonary oedema.

The experimental setup follows the CXR-LT benchmark, which was designed for long-tailed multi-label chest X-ray classification \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2025cxrlt2024}. Common findings appear frequently, while rare findings have very few positive examples. This imbalance can bias standard models toward frequent labels, so the evaluation reports both overall performance and frequency-group performance. Because this thesis also requires linked clinical text, vital signs, local image files, and prior-study information, the experiments are performed on a filtered CXR-LT-aligned cohort rather than the full public benchmark set.

The data are built from MIMIC-CXR (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care Chest X-ray), MIMIC-CXR-JPG, MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV), and MIMIC-IV-ED (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care IV Emergency Department) \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,johnson2019mimiccxrjpg,johnson2024mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. These sources provide chest X-ray images, radiology report sections, structured emergency-department vital signs, and patient-level links for prior studies.

\subsection{Clinical and Technical Challenges}\label{subsec}

This setting introduces five main challenges.

First, the model must avoid label leakage. The findings and impression sections of a radiology report are written after image interpretation, so using them as model inputs would create label leakage. This thesis therefore uses only pre-interpretation clinical text, mainly indication and history, as model input.

Second, the input modalities have different formats and different missingness patterns. Radiographs are high-dimensional images, indications are short free-text descriptions, vital signs are structured numerical variables, and prior studies are not available for every patient. These inputs must be encoded into a shared representation while preserving missingness information.

Third, the target labels are both imbalanced and correlated. Rare findings have few positive examples, and several findings can appear in the same study. The model must therefore handle class imbalance and label co-occurrence.

Fourth, prior-study information is useful but can introduce shortcuts. Previous examinations help clinicians judge whether a finding is new, stable, improved, or resolved. However, a model may over-rely on the prior study and pay too little attention to the current radiograph. The prior-aware component must therefore use prior context as auxiliary evidence rather than as the main prediction shortcut.

Finally, multimodal fusion increases computational cost. High-resolution image features can produce many tokens, and transformer attention scales quadratically with sequence length \cite{vaswani2017attention}. The implementation therefore uses compact token layouts and other design choices as practical implementations to manage computational and memory requirements.

\subsection{Approach Overview}\label{subsec}

We adapt the CaMCheX idea to evaluate whether clinical context and prior-study information improve long-tailed chest X-ray classification \cite{sloan2025camchex}. The model combines current chest X-ray images, pre-interpretation clinical text, structured vital signs, and prior-study context in one classification pipeline.

Each modality is encoded separately: a visual backbone encodes the radiographs, a pre-trained clinical language model encodes the pre-interpretation clinical text, projection layers encode vital signs, and temporal embeddings encode prior-study time gaps. We combine these features with a transformer-based fusion module. The model uses compact token layouts to reduce memory cost while keeping useful image and context features. We use prior-study context as auxiliary temporal evidence, so the model can compare studies without relying entirely on historical information.

Because multimodal models may improve metrics while still using weak or shortcut evidence, this thesis also includes qualitative attribution analysis. Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping) visualizations, text attribution, and modality-level summaries are used as sanity checks for selected predictions. These analyses are not treated as validated clinical explanations, but as qualitative evidence of model behaviour \cite{selvaraju2017gradcam}.

\subsection{Thesis Contributions}\label{subsec}

The main contributions are:\begin{itemize}\item A CaMCheX-inspired multimodal chest X-ray classification model that combines radiographs, pre-interpretation clinical text, structured vital signs, and prior-study context.\item An implementation of the multimodal architecture incorporating practical design choices such as reduced image resolution, frozen text encoders, and compact token layouts.\item A prior-aware temporal component that uses historical study information and time-gap embeddings while limiting over-reliance on prior information.\item An ablation study measuring the contribution of input modalities and architectural components, including prior context, label-correlation modelling, and channel encoding.\item Qualitative attribution analysis using Grad-CAM, text attribution, and modality-level summaries to inspect selected predictions.\end{itemize}
