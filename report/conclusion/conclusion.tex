\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis addresses the challenging problem of long-tailed chest X-ray classification in ED settings. In clinical practice, chest radiographs are rarely interpreted in isolation; clinical indications, patient vital signs, and historical prior examinations are critical context. However, standard computer vision models typically rely solely on the current visual image, discarding these valuable non-visual clinical streams and struggle with the severe class imbalance of rare pathologies.

To resolve this limitation, this thesis proposes a prior-aware multimodal fusion framework that mirrors the diagnostic reasoning of clinicians. The model processes the target study (chest radiographs, clinical indication text, and vital signs) through modality-specific encoders and fuses them using a target transformer self-attention block. When historical prior imaging and clinical reports are available, they are encoded into a memory stream, which the target stream queries using a temporal cross-attention mechanism augmented by discretized time-gap embeddings. To ensure robustness to missing prior examinations, stochastic prior dropout is incorporated during training.

Experimental evaluations on the long-tailed CXR-LT dataset show that the integration of multimodal clinical contexts leads to a substantial improvement in diagnostic accuracy compared to image-only models. More importantly, the addition of prior-study context yields an additional performance leap, with the overall AP rising from $0.3246$ (for the image-only baseline) to $0.5595$ (for the proposed framework), and overall AUROC reaching $0.9014$. The largest relative gains are observed on rare tail classes, indicating that text indications and historical baselines provide crucial stabilization signals when visual samples are scarce. Furthermore, qualitative visualizations using Grad-CAM demonstrate that the model aligns with clinical practice, focusing on relevant anatomical regions in both target and prior images and key clinical terms in text records.

While this work is subject to limitations---such as a single-center cohort, reduced image resolution, and a frozen text encoder due to compute constraints---the results demonstrate the feasibility and power of prior-aware multimodal fusion. Future work will investigate end-to-end training of all modalities, processing high-resolution visual inputs, and validating the framework on external cohorts from different healthcare networks. In conclusion, this study validates that integrating clinical context and historical prior studies is a highly effective methodology for improving automated chest X-ray interpretation and addressing the challenges of long-tailed clinical datasets.
