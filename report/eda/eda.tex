
\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}

We first analyze the dataset structure before training the model.

\subsection{Dataset Cohort and Subsets}
\label{subsec:eda_cohort}

We construct the dataset from chest X-ray studies, radiology reports, and linked emergency department records. Chest X-ray images are taken from MIMIC-CXR-JPG, the processed JPG release derived from MIMIC-CXR, while radiology reports are obtained from MIMIC-CXR and vital signs are linked from MIMIC-IV ED \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,johnson2024mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. Each sample corresponds to a current chest X-ray study with CXR-LT target labels \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet} and, when available, clinical indication text, structured vital signs, and prior-study information.

Figure~\ref{fig:data_construction_pipeline} summarizes the construction of one multimodal sample. Diagnostions, namely Findings and Impression, are used only for label construction. In contrast, Indication and History are treated as pre-interpretation clinical text inputs. The 26 CXR-LT labels combine 14 original labels from MIMIC-CXR-JPG with 12 additional long-tail labels mined from report text using RadText. Images, text inputs, labels, and vital signs are then linked into the final multimodal sample.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/data-construction-pipeline.pdf}
    \caption{Construction of the multimodal sample. MIMIC-CXR-JPG provides chest X-ray images and the 14 original labels, while MIMIC-CXR reports provide both pre-interpretation text inputs and diagnostic sections used for label mining. Findings and Impression are used only for label construction, whereas Indication and History are used as model text inputs. The final sample combines the image, clinical text, vital signs, and the 26 CXR-LT target labels.}
    \label{fig:data_construction_pipeline}
\end{figure}

We build the cohort through three filtering steps. First, chest X-ray studies are selected according to the target CXR-LT classification setting. Second, studies are matched with available clinical information, including indication/history text and structured vital signs. Third, prior-study information is identified for patients who have previous chest X-ray examinations before the current study. Missing modalities are handled using the preprocessing strategy described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

\subsection{Label Distribution and Co-occurrence}
\label{subsec:eda_labels}

The target classification task is multi-label, where each study can contain multiple co-occurring findings, and long-tailed, meaning some findings occur far more frequently than others \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet}. Under the Chest X-Ray Long-Tailed (CXR-LT) benchmark formulation, the label space consists of 26 target pathologies. These findings comprise 14 original labels inherited from the standard CheXpert labeling system and 12 newly added labels extracted to specifically represent rare conditions and enrich the long-tailed evaluation. Figure~\ref{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded} shows the full CXR-LT label distribution and confirms the strong imbalance between common and rare findings.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/cxr-lt-2023-newlyadded-original-demonstrate.pdf}
    \caption[Frequency distribution of pathology classes in the CXR-LT benchmark]{Frequency distribution of the 26 pathology classes in the full CXR-LT benchmark source dataset, categorized by original CheXpert classes (blue) and newly added classes (red). Counts reflect the full benchmark population, not the filtered thesis subset.}
    \label{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded}
\end{figure}



\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/eda/logscale-label-count-distribution.png}
    \caption{Global label-count distribution of the 26 CXR-LT target labels on a log-scaled positive-rate axis, grouped into head, medium, and tail frequency tiers. Bar annotations report the raw positive count and positive rate for each class.}
    \label{fig:logscale_label_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:logscale_label_distribution} shows the global distribution of all 26 CXR-LT target labels using a log-scaled positive-rate axis. The distribution is strongly long-tailed: common findings such as Support Devices, Lung Opacity, Cardiomegaly, Pleural Effusion, and Atelectasis occur far more frequently than rare findings such as Pneumomediastinum, Pleural Other, and Pneumoperitoneum. The head, medium, and tail grouping makes this imbalance explicit and motivates the use of asymmetric loss, label-frequency-aware evaluation, and separate head/medium/tail analysis.

In addition to individual label frequencies, label co-occurrence is also important.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/label_cooccurrence.png}
    \caption{Co-occurrence matrix of target pathology labels.}
    \label{fig:label_cooccurrence}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:label_cooccurrence} shows the co-occurrence rates between different target pathology labels. High co-occurrence is observed between findings like cardiomegaly and edema, or pleural effusion and atelectasis, consistent with clinical pathophysiology where these findings represent linked disease manifestations. These correlations motivate a classifier head that models label relationships instead of treating every label independently.

\subsection{Clinical Indication Text Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_text}

Clinical indication text indicates the reason for imaging (e.g., symptoms or clinical queries) and is available at the time of examination. Unlike radiology reports written post-interpretation, indication text represents realistic input context \cite{sloan2025camchex}. To prevent leakage, we exclude post-imaging findings and impressions, and keep only pre-imaging indications and history.

\subsection{Temporal Prior Availability and Label Dynamics}
\label{subsec:eda_prior_gaps}

Prior-study context supports comparative diagnosis by enabling temporal comparison over time \cite{bannur2023biovilt}. However, integrating this information requires addressing two main challenges: handling cases where no prior study is available and accounting for variable time gaps between current and historical studies.



\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Availability of prior studies at study level in the full CXR-LT source dataset, broken down by data split.}
    \label{tab:prior_availability}
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \hline
        Split & With prior & Without prior & Total & \% With prior \\
        \hline
        Development & 15,736 & 6,532 & 22,268 & 70.67\% \\
        Test & 32,398 & 13,064 & 45,462 & 71.26\% \\
        Train & 114,195 & 45,733 & 159,928 & 71.40\% \\
        \hline
        Total & 162,329 & 65,329 & 227,658 & 71.30\% \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:prior_availability} shows the availability of prior chest X-ray examinations at the study level in the full CXR-LT source dataset (227,658 studies), broken down by data split. Overall, 71.30\% of studies have a linked historical prior. The proportion is consistent across splits (approximately 71\%), confirming that the train/validation/test partition does not introduce systematic bias in prior availability. This prevalence supports using prior-aware components, but the model must still handle the 28.70\% of studies without priors.



\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/prior_gap_distribution.png}
    \caption[Distribution of time gaps between current and prior studies]{Distribution of time gaps between current and prior chest X-ray studies (in days, log-scaled).}
    \label{fig:prior_gap_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:prior_gap_distribution} shows the wide dynamic range of time gaps between current and prior studies, exhibiting peaks representing acute follow-ups (1 to 30 days) and routine screenings (approximately 365 days). This wide variation motivates discretizing prior-study time gaps into learned temporal buckets.

Because time gaps are skewed, we bucket the time difference and learn an embedding for each bucket.

Beyond prior-study availability and time-gap distribution, it is important to examine whether prior studies contain useful temporal signal for the target labels. If labels frequently remain unchanged between a prior and current examination, historical context may help the model recognize persistent or chronic findings. However, if labels often appear or disappear between studies, the model must still rely on the current radiograph and clinical context rather than simply copying prior information.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/eda/label-transition-prior-current.png}
    \caption[Temporal label agreement and transitions between current and prior studies]{
    Temporal label agreement and positive-label transitions between current and prior chest X-ray studies.
    The left panel reports the probability that a label has the same binary value in the current and prior
    study, including both positive-positive and negative-negative agreement. The right panel separates
    positive-label transitions into newly appearing findings (prior negative to current positive) and resolved
    or lost findings (prior positive to current negative). High agreement for rare labels should be interpreted
    carefully, as it is often driven by negative-negative agreement rather than true disease persistence.
    }
    \label{fig:temporal_label_dynamics}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal_label_dynamics} shows that many labels exhibit high agreement between prior and current studies. However, we should interpret this agreement carefully.

The transition plot provides a more informative view of temporal change. Common findings such as Lung Opacity, Atelectasis, Cardiomegaly, Pneumonia, and Pleural Effusion show larger fractions of both newly positive and lost positive transitions. This suggests that prior context is useful but insufficient on its own: some findings are stable across time, while others evolve between examinations. These observations motivate prior-aware temporal fusion and support design choices such as time-gap embeddings and prior dropout, which reduce over-dependence on historical information.
