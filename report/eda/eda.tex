
\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}

This section presents an exploratory analysis of the dataset used in this thesis. The goal of this analysis is to understand the structure of the multimodal data before model development. Since the task combines chest X-ray images, clinical indication text, structured vital signs, and prior-study information, it is important to examine not only the label distribution but also the availability and quality of each input modality.

\subsection{Dataset Cohort and Subsets}
\label{subsec:eda_cohort}

The dataset used in this thesis is constructed from chest X-ray studies and associated clinical information. The image component is derived from MIMIC-CXR \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr}, while the clinical context is obtained from associated emergency department and hospital records \cite{johnson2023mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. Each sample corresponds to a current chest X-ray study with available target labels defined by the CXR-LT setting \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet} and, when possible, linked clinical indication text, vital signs, and prior-study information.

The cohort construction process follows several filtering steps. First, chest X-ray studies are selected according to the target classification setting. Second, studies are matched with available clinical information, including indication text and structured vital signs. Third, prior-study information is identified for patients who have previous chest X-ray examinations before the current study. Cases with missing modalities are handled according to the preprocessing strategy described in Section~\ref{subsec:method_preprocessing}.

For modelling, these statistics show that the proposed model must accommodate incomplete vital sign inputs while maintaining stable training. Furthermore, patient-level splits prevent data leakage across training and validation sets.

\subsection{Label Distribution and Co-occurrence}
\label{subsec:eda_labels}

The target classification task is multi-label, where each study can contain multiple co-occurring findings, and long-tailed, meaning some findings occur far more frequently than others \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet}. This class imbalance presents a challenge where standard training objectives may bias predictions toward common pathologies at the expense of rare, clinically significant findings.

Figure~\ref{fig:label_distribution} shows the number of positive samples for each target label.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/label_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of positive labels across the target chest X-ray findings.}
    \label{fig:label_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:label_distribution} shows a strong long-tailed label distribution. Common findings such as pleural effusion, atelectasis, and cardiomegaly appear much more frequently than rare findings such as pneumothorax, fracture, and hernia, with the imbalance ratio exceeding 100:1. For modelling, this severe class imbalance motivates the use of imbalance-aware loss functions, such as asymmetric loss, and separate evaluation of frequent and rare labels to prevent predictions from being dominated by majority negative classes.

In addition to individual label frequencies, label co-occurrence is also important. Figure~\ref{fig:label_cooccurrence} presents a co-occurrence matrix between target findings.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/label_cooccurrence.png}
    \caption{Co-occurrence matrix of target pathology labels.}
    \label{fig:label_cooccurrence}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:label_cooccurrence} shows the co-occurrence rates between different target pathology labels. High co-occurrence is observed between findings like cardiomegaly and edema, or pleural effusion and atelectasis, which is consistent with clinical pathophysiology where these findings represent linked disease manifestations. For modelling, these strong label correlations suggest that classification performance can be improved by employing models that represent joint label relationships, rather than predicting each condition as a completely independent binary variable.

\subsection{Clinical Indication Text Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_text}

Clinical indication text indicates the reason for imaging (e.g., symptoms or clinical queries) and is available at the time of examination. Unlike radiology reports written post-interpretation, indication text represents realistic input context \cite{sloan2025camchex}. The text modality varies in length and content, ranging from short symptoms to detailed clinical summaries.

Figure~\ref{fig:text_length_distribution} shows the distribution of clinical indication text lengths.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/text_length_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of clinical indication text lengths (in words).}
    \label{fig:text_length_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:text_length_distribution} shows the distribution of clinical indication text lengths, indicating a median length of approximately 12 words with a long tail extending past 50 words. For modelling, this right-skewed distribution suggests that a relatively short text sequence length is sufficient to capture most clinical indication details, allowing the text encoder to use a moderate maximum token limit to preserve context while minimizing computational costs.

\subsection{Vital Signs and Missingness Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_vitals}

Structured vital signs represent physiological states (e.g., temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation) recorded near the time of imaging. To utilize incomplete physiological records without discarding studies, this thesis employs a missingness-aware representation that combines imputed values with explicit missingness masks.

For modelling, these extreme values motivate the implementation of robust preprocessing, such as outlier clipping and training-set-based z-score normalization, to prevent extreme values from distorting the model's feature space.

\subsection{Temporal Prior Gaps}
\label{subsec:eda_prior_gaps}

Prior-study context supports comparative diagnosis by enabling temporal comparison over time \cite{bannur2023biovilt}. However, integrating this information requires addressing two main challenges: handling cases where no prior study is available and accounting for variable time gaps between current and historical studies.

Table~\ref{tab:prior_availability} summarizes the availability of prior studies in the dataset.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Availability of prior studies at study level.}
    \label{tab:prior_availability}
    \begin{tabular}{lrr}
        \hline
        Prior-study status & Count & Percentage \\
        \hline
        With prior study & 14,045 & 82.24\% \\
        Without prior study & 3,033 & 17.76\% \\
        Total & 17,078 & 100.00\% \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:prior_availability} shows the availability of prior chest X-ray examinations at the study level, indicating that 82.24\% of studies have a linked historical prior. For modelling, this high prevalence demonstrates that prior studies provide a frequent source of temporal context, justifying the inclusion of prior-aware components in the classification architecture while requiring robustness to the 17.76\% of cases where no prior is available.

Figure~\ref{fig:prior_gap_distribution} shows the distribution of time gaps between current and prior studies.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/prior_gap_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of time gaps between current and prior chest X-ray studies (in days, log-scaled).}
    \label{fig:prior_gap_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:prior_gap_distribution} shows the wide dynamic range of time gaps between current and prior studies, exhibiting peaks representing acute follow-ups (1 to 30 days) and routine screenings (approximately 365 days). For modelling, this wide variation suggests that the clinical relevance of a prior study may change depending on its temporal distance, motivating the discretization of time gaps into learned embedding buckets rather than assuming a simple linear relationship.

Because the time-gap distribution may be skewed, this thesis discretizes the time difference into temporal buckets before adding it to the model as a learned embedding. This allows the model to represent approximate temporal distance without assuming that time differences have a simple linear effect.
