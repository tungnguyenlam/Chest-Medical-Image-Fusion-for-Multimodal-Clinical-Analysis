
\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}

This section describes how the thesis dataset is constructed and why its structure matters for the modelling choices used later. We first explain how the multimodal samples are built, then analyze label imbalance, clinical text, vital-sign missingness, and prior-study availability.

\subsection{Dataset Cohort and Subsets}
\label{subsec:eda_cohort}

The thesis cohort is constructed by linking chest X-ray studies, radiology reports, and emergency department records. Chest X-ray images are taken from MIMIC-CXR-JPG, which is the processed JPG release of MIMIC-CXR. Radiology reports are taken from MIMIC-CXR, and vital signs are linked from MIMIC-IV ED \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,johnson2024mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. Each sample represents a current chest X-ray study with CXR-LT target labels \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet} and, when available, clinical indication text, structured vital signs, and prior-study information.

Figure~\ref{fig:data_construction_pipeline} summarizes the construction of one multimodal sample. The diagnostic report sections, namely Findings and Impression, are used only for label construction. In contrast, Indication and History are treated as pre-interpretation text inputs and are used by the model. The 26 CXR-LT labels combine 14 original MIMIC-CXR-JPG labels with 12 additional long-tail labels mined from report text using RadText. These image, text, label, and vital-sign components are then linked to form the final multimodal sample.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/data-construction-pipeline.pdf}
    \caption{Construction of the multimodal sample.}
    \label{fig:data_construction_pipeline}
\end{figure}

The filtered thesis cohort is built in three steps. First, we select chest X-ray studies that match the CXR-LT classification task. Second, we match these studies with available clinical information, including indication/history text and structured vital signs. Third, we identify prior chest X-ray studies from the same patient when they exist before the current study. Missing modalities are not removed automatically. They are handled using the preprocessing strategy described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

Structured vital signs are available for 24,209 of 28,245 image-level rows, so they are missing for approximately 14.3\% of rows. The model must therefore handle missing vital signs during training. Patient-level splits prevent the same patient from appearing in more than one split.

The dataset contains multiple chest X-ray view positions, so image views must be grouped before visual encoding. AP means anteroposterior, PA means posteroanterior, and LL means left lateral. In this thesis, AP and PA are treated as frontal views, while LATERAL and LL are treated as lateral views. Lateral views are also frequent. This supports the use of a multi-view image pipeline rather than assuming that each study contains only one image.

\subsection{Label Distribution and Co-occurrence}
\label{subsec:eda_labels}

The target task is challenging because it is both multi-label and long-tailed. In this setting, one study can contain several findings, and some findings appear much more often than others \cite{holste2024cxrlt,holste2023cxrlt_physionet}. The CXR-LT benchmark uses 26 target findings. These 26 findings include 14 original MIMIC-CXR-JPG labels and 12 additional rare labels mined from report text. Figure~\ref{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded} shows the raw label counts in the full CXR-LT source dataset. The figure highlights the large frequency gap between common and rare findings.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/cxr-lt-2023-newlyadded-original-demonstrate.pdf}
    \caption[Frequency distribution of pathology classes in the CXR-LT benchmark]{Frequency distribution of CXR-LT target labels.}
    \label{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded}
\end{figure}

In the figure, blue labels indicate original MIMIC-CXR-JPG labels, while red labels indicate additional long-tail labels. These counts describe the full CXR-LT source dataset, not the filtered thesis cohort.



\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/eda/logscale-label-count-distribution.png}
    \caption{Label-count distribution by frequency tier.}
    \label{fig:logscale_label_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:logscale_label_distribution} shows the same long-tailed pattern using head, medium, and tail groups. The positive-rate axis is log-scaled, and each bar reports the raw positive count and positive rate for one class. Common findings such as Support Devices, Lung Opacity, Cardiomegaly, Pleural Effusion, and Atelectasis appear far more often than rare findings such as Pneumomediastinum, Pleural Other, and Pneumoperitoneum. This imbalance motivates three choices used later in the thesis: asymmetric loss during training, mAP and mAUROC reporting by frequency group, and separate analysis for head, medium, and tail labels.

Beyond individual label frequency, label co-occurrence is also important because several findings can appear in the same study.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/label_cooccurrence.png}
    \caption{Co-occurrence matrix of the target labels.}
    \label{fig:label_cooccurrence}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:label_cooccurrence} shows label co-occurrence in the dataset. Each cell shows the conditional probability that the column label is positive when the row label is positive. The diagonal is 1 by definition. Some labels often appear together. For example, Cardiomegaly often co-occurs with Edema, and Pleural Effusion often co-occurs with Atelectasis. These pairs are clinically plausible because they can appear in related disease processes. These correlations motivate the label-correlation head described in Section~4.10.

\subsection{Clinical Indication Text Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_text}

Clinical indication text provides the reason for ordering the chest X-ray, such as symptoms or a rule-out question. It is available before the radiologist writes the final report. Therefore, indication and history text are realistic inputs for the model \cite{sloan2025camchex}. To prevent label leakage, we exclude Findings and Impression from the model input. Only Indication and History are kept as text inputs.

\subsection{Vital Signs and Missingness Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_vitals}

Vital signs provide useful clinical context, but they are not available for every study. To keep these studies, we use imputed values together with explicit missingness masks.

    \label{tab:vitals_summary}

        \hline
        Temperature & 98.15 & 3.37 & 98.20 & 1.04 & 105.40 & 17.18 \\
        Heart rate & 88.53 & 19.48 & 88.00 & 7.00 & 200.00 & 14.08 \\

        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:vitals_summary} reports vital-sign statistics in the training split. Extreme values are kept in the table to show why outlier handling is needed. The vital-sign variables contain both missing values and extreme outliers. Some values are not physiologically plausible, such as an oxygen saturation of 921.00 or a diastolic blood pressure of 7,698.90 mmHg. These values likely come from data-entry errors or raw clinical outliers. Therefore, Section~4 applies outlier clipping and z-score normalization using training-set statistics.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/vitals_missingness.png}
    \caption{Missingness rate by variable and data split.}
    \label{fig:vitals_missingness}

Figure~\ref{fig:vitals_missingness} shows that most vital-sign variables have similar missingness rates, around 14--17\%. Clinical indication has 0\% missingness because it was required during cohort filtering. This suggests that vital signs are often recorded or missing as a group. This supports representing vital signs with a combined vital-sign token and an explicit missingness mask.

\subsection{Temporal Prior Availability and Label Dynamics}
\label{subsec:eda_prior_gaps}

Prior-study context allows the model to compare the current chest X-ray with earlier examinations \cite{bannur2023biovilt}. However, this source of context introduces two practical issues. Some studies have no prior chest X-ray, and the time gap between the current and prior study can vary from days to years.



\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Availability of prior studies.}
    \label{tab:prior_availability}
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \hline
        Split & With prior & Without prior & Total & \% With prior \\
        \hline
        Validation (development split) & 15,736 & 6,532 & 22,268 & 70.67\% \\
        Test & 32,398 & 13,064 & 45,462 & 71.26\% \\
        Train & 114,195 & 45,733 & 159,928 & 71.40\% \\
        \hline
        Total & 162,329 & 65,329 & 227,658 & 71.30\% \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:prior_availability} shows prior-study availability at study level in the full CXR-LT source dataset before the thesis filtering step. These totals describe the full source dataset, not the filtered thesis cohort in Table~\ref{tab:cohort_summary}. Overall, 71.30\% of studies have a linked prior chest X-ray. The percentage is similar across splits, around 71\%. This suggests that prior availability is balanced across train, validation, and test splits. This supports using prior-study information, but the model must still work for the 28.70\% of studies without priors.



\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/prior_gap_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of time gaps between current and prior studies.}
    \label{fig:prior_gap_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:prior_gap_distribution} shows the time gap between current and prior chest X-ray studies, measured in days. The x-axis is log-scaled. The time gaps vary widely. Some priors are close to the current study, while others are months or years earlier. This motivates using learned time-gap buckets in the prior-aware fusion module.

Prior availability alone does not show whether the prior study is actually useful. It is also necessary to examine whether labels stay the same or change between prior and current studies. If labels often stay the same, prior information may help the model. If labels often appear or disappear, the model must still rely on the current image and current clinical context.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/eda/label-transition-prior-current.png}
    \caption[Temporal label agreement and transitions between current and prior studies]{Temporal label agreement and positive-label transitions.}
    \label{fig:temporal_label_dynamics}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal_label_dynamics} compares labels between prior and current chest X-ray studies. The left panel shows how often each label has the same binary value in the prior and current study. This includes both positive-positive and negative-negative agreement. The right panel shows labels that newly appear and labels that disappear. High agreement for rare labels should be interpreted carefully because it is often caused by negative-negative agreement.

Many labels have high agreement between prior and current studies. However, this does not always mean that disease is stable. The transition plot is more useful for understanding actual label changes. Common findings such as Lung Opacity, Atelectasis, Cardiomegaly, Pneumonia, and Pleural Effusion show many newly positive and lost positive transitions. This suggests that prior context is useful, but it cannot be used as the only evidence. Some findings stay stable, while others change between studies. Together, these observations motivate the prior-aware temporal fusion design used later in the methodology. They also support using time-gap embeddings and prior dropout, so the model does not simply copy the prior study.
