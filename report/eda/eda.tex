
\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
\label{sec:eda}

This section details the construction of the multimodal chest X-ray cohort and outlines key dataset characteristics that inform subsequent modelling choices. We present the sample assembly process, followed by an analysis of label imbalance, clinical text properties, vital-sign missingness, and prior-study temporal dynamics.

\subsection{Dataset Cohort and Subsets}
\label{subsec:eda_cohort}

The dataset cohort is established by aligning chest X-ray studies, radiology reports, and emergency department (ED) records. Specifically, chest X-ray images are sourced from the MIMIC-CXR-JPG database, paired with radiology reports from MIMIC-CXR, and matched with vital signs from MIMIC-IV ED \cite{johnson2019mimiccxr,johnson2024mimiciv,johnson2023mimicived}. Each resulting sample represents a unique, current chest X-ray study mapped to 26 CXR-LT-2023 target labels \cite{holste2024cxrlt,PhysioNet-cxr-lt-iccv-workshop-cvamd-1.0.0}, integrated with pre-interpretation clinical indications, structured vitals, and longitudinal prior studies where available.

Figure~\ref{fig:data_construction_pipeline} illustrates the construction pipeline for a single multimodal sample. To prevent label leakage, diagnostic report sections (\textit{Findings} and \textit{Impression}) are reserved solely for ground-truth label extraction, whereas pre-interpretation text (\textit{Indication} and \textit{History}) is utilized as model input. The 26 CXR-LT-2023 target classes combine the 14 standard MIMIC-CXR findings with 12 additional long-tail labels extracted from report texts using RadText. These image, text, label, and vital-sign modalities are aligned by study and patient identifiers to form the final training samples.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/data-construction-pipeline.pdf}
    \caption{Construction of the multimodal sample.}
    \label{fig:data_construction_pipeline}
\end{figure}

The cohort filtering and construction proceeds in three steps: (1) selecting chest X-ray studies corresponding to the CXR-LT-2023 classification task; (2) aligning these studies with clinical metadata (indication text and structured vital signs); and (3) retrieving prior chest X-ray studies for the patient when available. Partially missing modalities are preserved rather than filtered out, and are addressed using the robust imputation and masking strategies detailed in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

The model must therefore robustly accommodate missing clinical vitals during training. The splits are partitioned at the patient level to prevent data leakage across train, validation, and test subsets.

Because each chest X-ray study can contain multiple acquisitions, view positions must be categorized prior to visual encoding. Anteroposterior (AP) and posteroanterior (PA) acquisitions are grouped as frontal views, while lateral (LATERAL) and left lateral (LL) acquisitions are grouped as lateral views. Figure~\ref{fig:view_position_distribution} illustrates the view distribution across the dataset. The dominance of frontal views, coupled with a significant presence of lateral acquisitions, supports a multi-view input pipeline that processes multiple views per study rather than assuming single-image studies.

\subsection{Label Distribution and Co-occurrence}
\label{subsec:eda_labels}

The target task exhibits both multi-label and long-tailed characteristics, presenting a significant clinical and technical challenge. Individual studies often present co-occurring pathologies, while the prevalence of these pathologies varies by several orders of magnitude \cite{holste2024cxrlt,PhysioNet-cxr-lt-iccv-workshop-cvamd-1.0.0}. The 26 target findings of the CXR-LT-2023 benchmark are comprised of 14 standard MIMIC-CXR classes and 12 additional long-tail categories mined from reports. Figure~\ref{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded} shows the overall label counts, highlighting the pronounced frequency disparity between common (head) and rare (tail) pathologies.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/cxr-lt-2023-newlyadded-original-demonstrate.pdf}
    \caption[Frequency distribution of pathology classes in the CXR-LT-2023 benchmark]{Frequency distribution of CXR-LT-2023 target labels.}
    \label{fig:cxrlt_original_newlyadded}
\end{figure}

Here, blue bars denote standard MIMIC-CXR categories, whereas red bars denote the newly integrated long-tail labels. These statistics reflect the raw distribution before cohort filtering.



\begin{figure}[!htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{img/eda/logscale-label-count-distribution.png}
    \caption{Label-count distribution by frequency tier.}
    \label{fig:logscale_label_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:logscale_label_distribution} presents the log-scaled positive rates stratified into head, medium, and tail frequency tiers. Common conditions (e.g., \textit{Support Devices}, \textit{Lung Opacity}, \textit{Cardiomegaly}) exhibit prevalence rates orders of magnitude higher than rare findings (e.g., \textit{Pneumomediastinum}, \textit{Pleural Other}, \textit{Pneumoperitoneum}). This severe imbalance motivates three critical design choices in this work: the use of asymmetric loss (ASL) during training, the evaluation of mAP and mAUROC metrics across distinct frequency tiers, and stratified validation analysis.

Beyond marginal frequencies, modeling pairwise correlations is essential since multiple pathologies frequently co-occur within a single clinical presentation.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/label_cooccurrence.png}
    \caption{Co-occurrence matrix of the target labels.}
    \label{fig:label_cooccurrence}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:label_cooccurrence} visualizes the conditional probabilities of co-occurrence, where each cell represents the probability of the column label given the row label. Strong clinical correlations are visible; for instance, \textit{Cardiomegaly} co-occurs frequently with \textit{Edema}, and \textit{Pleural Effusion} with \textit{Atelectasis}. These patterns align with clinical expectations, as these pathologies are pathophysiologically linked.

\subsection{Clinical Indication Text Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_text}

The clinical indication text details the pre-imaging symptoms, history, or diagnostic questions prompting the examination. Because this text is available prior to image interpretation, it serves as a realistic input for clinical decision support systems \cite{sloan2025camchex}. To prevent label leakage, we strictly exclude the \textit{Findings} and \textit{Impression} sections from model inputs, restricting the text stream to \textit{Indication} and \textit{History}.

\subsection{Vital Signs and Missingness Analysis}
\label{subsec:eda_vitals}

Structured vital signs provide immediate physiological context but are subject to missingness in clinical practice. Rather than discarding incomplete samples, we employ a joint representation combining imputed values with explicit missingness indicator masks.

Figure~\ref{fig:vitals_missingness} shows uniform missingness rates (14--17\%) across the vital sign variables within each split, suggesting that vitals are typically documented or omitted as a bundle. In contrast, clinical indication exhibits 0\% missingness due to cohort filtering. This co-missingness behavior supports encoding vitals via a unified vector paired with a binary missingness mask.

\subsection{Temporal Prior Availability and Label Dynamics}
\label{subsec:eda_prior_gaps}

Incorporating longitudinal prior studies allows models to detect dynamic disease progression \cite{bannur2023biovilt}. However, leveraging this context introduces two main challenges: a subset of patients have no historical imaging available, and the elapsed time between the current and prior acquisitions ranges from days to years.



\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Availability of prior studies.}
    \label{tab:prior_availability}
    \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
        \hline
        Split & With prior & Without prior & Total & \% With prior \\
        \hline
        Validation (development split) & 15,736 & 6,532 & 22,268 & 70.67\% \\
        Test & 32,398 & 13,064 & 45,462 & 71.26\% \\
        Train & 114,195 & 45,733 & 159,928 & 71.40\% \\
        \hline
        Total & 162,329 & 65,329 & 227,658 & 71.30\% \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:prior_availability} outlines prior-study availability in the source CXR-LT-2023 dataset. Approximately 71.30\% of all studies have an associated prior chest X-ray, with consistent availability (~71\%) across all splits. While this consistent distribution supports integrating longitudinal context, the model must remain robust to the remaining 28.70\% of cases lacking historical images.



\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{img/prior_gap_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of time gaps between current and prior studies.}
    \label{fig:prior_gap_distribution}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:prior_gap_distribution} displays the log-scaled distribution of the temporal gap (in days) between the current and prior studies. The wide variance, spanning immediate follow-ups to multi-year intervals, motivates the use of learned temporal bucket embeddings within the prior-aware fusion architecture.

However, prior availability alone does not guarantee utility; we must analyze how pathology labels evolve over time. High temporal stability suggests that priors provide strong predictive priors, whereas frequent label transitions require the model to rely more heavily on current imaging and clinical context.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{img/eda/label-transition-prior-current.png}
    \caption[Temporal label agreement and transitions between current and prior studies]{Temporal label agreement and positive-label transitions.}
    \label{fig:temporal_label_dynamics}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:temporal_label_dynamics} characterizes this longitudinal label behavior. The left panel shows the overall binary agreement (concordance of both positive and negative states) between studies, while the right panel tracks state transitions (newly onset or resolved pathologies). Notably, the high agreement observed for rare categories is largely driven by negative-negative agreement and must be interpreted cautiously.

While overall agreement is high, the transition dynamics reveal significant clinical variation. Common acute findings (e.g., \textit{Lung Opacity}, \textit{Atelectasis}, \textit{Pneumonia}, \textit{Pleural Effusion}) undergo frequent transitions. This underscores that while historical context is highly informative, the model must not over-rely on it at the expense of current observations. These findings directly motivate the prior-aware temporal fusion network design, including the use of time-gap embeddings and prior dropout to prevent the model from shortcutting and simply copying prior predictions.
