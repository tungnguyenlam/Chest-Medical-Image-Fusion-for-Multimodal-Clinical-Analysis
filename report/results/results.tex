\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the evaluation of the proposed multimodal fusion models on the long-tailed CXR-LT dataset. It first details the experimental configuration and hyperparameter settings used to train the models. It then presents the main quantitative results comparing the prior-aware multimodal framework against image-only and standard multimodal baselines. Next, an ablation study analyzes the individual impact of each model component, followed by a detailed per-class and long-tail analysis. Finally, a qualitative analysis of the model's predictions using Grad-CAM attributions across different input modalities is provided.

\subsection{Main Performance of the Proposed Model}
\label{subsec:results_quantitative}

The performance of all models is evaluated using two primary metrics: mAP and mAUROC. Given the severe long-tailed distribution of the 26 pathology classes, the performance breakdown is reported across three frequency-based categories:
\textbf{Head classes} are highly frequent pathologies, e.g., Support Devices, Pleural Effusion, and Cardiomegaly.

\textbf{Medium classes} are moderately frequent findings, e.g., Atelectasis, Pneumothorax, and Edema.

\textbf{Tail classes} represent rare findings, e.g., Hernia, Pneumomediastinum, and Fibrosis.

Table~\ref{tab:ours_main_results} compares the performance of the three model configurations trained and evaluated on the dataset in this thesis.

\begin{table}
    \centering
    \caption{Main test-set performance of the implemented models in this thesis.}
    \label{tab:ours_main_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \hline
        Model & mAP & AUROC & Head mAP & Tail mAP \\
        \hline
        Image-only baseline & 0.3246 & 0.8123 & 0.5841 & 0.0827 \\
        Multimodal without prior & 0.4098 & 0.8732 & 0.7116 & 0.1586 \\
        Proposed prior-aware multimodal model & 0.5595 & 0.9014 & 0.7742 & 0.3893 \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The quantitative comparisons demonstrate several key insights:
\textbf{Benefit of Multimodal Fusion}: Moving from an image-only model to the multimodal CaMCheX baseline yields a substantial improvement of $+0.0852$ absolute mAP ($0.3246 \rightarrow 0.4098$) and $+0.0609$ absolute AUROC ($0.8123 \rightarrow 0.8732$). This highlights the diagnostic utility of combining radiographs with ED clinical text and patient vital signs.

\textbf{Impact of Prior-Aware Modeling}: Incorporating historical radiographic and textual context leads to another significant performance leap. The proposed prior-aware framework achieves an overall mAP of $0.5595$ (an absolute increase of $+0.1497$ absolute mAP over the standard multimodal baseline) and an overall AUROC of $0.9014$.

\textbf{Addressing Tail Class Performance}: Long-tailed categories (tail classes) show the most dramatic relative improvements under the prior-aware model, with mAP rising from $0.1586 \rightarrow 0.3893$ ($+0.2307$ absolute mAP) and AUROC increasing to $0.9173$. This indicates that historical summaries and temporal comparisons provide critical stabilization signals for diagnosing rare pathologies.

The ablation results highlight several critical aspects of the model's architecture:
\textbf{Modality Contributions}: Integrating clinical text and vital signs with chest radiographs provides the largest single gain ($0.8123 \rightarrow 0.8732$ Macro AUROC), proving that diagnostic decisions benefit significantly from non-visual patient context.

\textbf{Value of Historical Prior}: Adding prior study tokens via cross-attention yields a further boost ($0.8732 \rightarrow 0.8985$ Macro AUROC). This confirms that temporal comparison behaves as a valuable anchor, especially when distinguishing acute changes from stable chronic conditions.

\textbf{Impact of Time-Gap Embeddings}: Removing the discretized time-gap embeddings degrades performance to $0.8845$, demonstrating that the model benefits from knowing the temporal distance of a historical study (e.g. a prior from 2 days ago is treated differently than a prior from 3 years ago).

\textbf{Prior Dropout Regularization}: Training without prior dropout (where prior information is randomly dropped during training) reduces test performance to $0.8901$. This suggests that stochastic prior dropout acts as a vital regularizer, preventing the network from developing shortcut dependencies on historical data and forcing the target stream representation to remain robust.

\textbf{Label Correlation Graph Head}: Introducing the directed, noise-aware label-correlation graph head to structure the ML-Decoder query representation yields a substantial improvement ($0.4950 \rightarrow 0.5595$ Macro mAP and $0.8985 \rightarrow 0.9014$ Macro AUROC). This indicates that representing joint label co-occurrences directly in the classifier head helps regularize the feature space and stabilizes the detection of correlated pathologies, especially under severe class imbalance.

\subsubsection{Input Channel Encoding Ablation}
\label{subsubsec:ablation_channels}

The image encoder expects a three-channel input, which leaves open the question of how a single-channel chest radiograph should be expanded to fill those channels. The proposed model uses an engineered multi-channel encoding, in which the three channels carry distinct, separately-normalized representations of the radiograph rather than a single replicated grayscale image. To test whether this hand-designed channel encoding is justified, it is compared against a plain \texttt{gray3} baseline, where the raw grayscale image (normalized with mean $0.4721$ and standard deviation $0.3037$) is simply replicated across all three channels. Both configurations share the same architecture, training recipe, and patient-level test split, and both are evaluated under full multimodal inputs as well as the report-dropped ablation.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_channels} reports the comparison. The engineered channel encoding and the plain \texttt{gray3} baseline are effectively tied: \texttt{gray3} is marginally higher on macro mAP ($0.5633$ vs.\ $0.5595$) and tail mAP ($0.4059$ vs.\ $0.3893$), while the engineered encoding is marginally higher on macro AUROC ($0.9014$ vs.\ $0.9000$). The report-dropped floor is likewise within noise of the two ($0.3367$ vs.\ $0.3412$ macro mAP).

The key takeaway is that the engineered multi-channel encoding provides no net benefit over simply replicating the grayscale image: the two configurations differ by at most a few thousandths of a point on every metric, with neither dominating. Given the added preprocessing cost and complexity of the engineered channels, the plain \texttt{gray3} encoding is preferable as the default, and the small differences observed are best attributed to run-to-run variance rather than a genuine advantage for either scheme. This indicates that, for this fusion framework, the diagnostic signal is carried by the multimodal context and architectural components rather than by hand-crafted channel-level image transformations.

\subsection{Long-Tail and Per-Class Analysis}
\label{subsec:results_longtail}

To understand how the proposed prior-aware model handles the severe class imbalance, this thesis analyzes performance across individual pathologies in the CXR-LT dataset. Long-tailed classification typically suffers because tail classes have very few positive examples, leading to poor generalization.

The findings indicate that the prior-aware multimodal framework achieves the most substantial improvements on tail classes, with their average AUROC increasing to $0.9173$ (compared to $0.7998$ for the image-only model). This behavior can be explained by two clinical reasons:
\textbf{Semantic Anchoring}: Rare findings such as Hernia, Pneumomediastinum, and Calcification of the Aorta have highly specific textual patterns in clinical indications (e.g., ``evaluate for hiatal hernia'' or ``rule out free air''). Fusing these textual cues compensates for the lack of visual examples in the training set.

\textbf{Temporal Baseline comparison}: For stable, chronic conditions that appear in the tail (such as Tortuous Aorta, Scoliosis, or Fibrosis), the model leverages the prior label and image to confirm that the finding has been historically present, reducing false positives and stabilizing predictions.
