\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the evaluation of the proposed multimodal fusion models on the long-tailed CXR-LT dataset. It first details the experimental configuration and hyperparameter settings used to train the models. It then presents the main quantitative results comparing the prior-aware multimodal framework against image-only and standard multimodal baselines. Next, an ablation study analyzes the individual impact of each model component, followed by a detailed per-class and long-tail analysis. Finally, a qualitative analysis of the model's predictions using Grad-CAM attributions across different input modalities is provided.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:results_setup}

This section details the experimental configuration, hyperparameters, and evaluation settings used to train and test the proposed models:

\textbf{Dataset Split}: The cohort is split at the patient level into training (11,941 studies, 19,556 images), validation (1,804 studies, 3,047 images), and test (3,333 studies, 5,642 images) subsets. Patient-level splitting ensures that no patient has images in both the training and evaluation sets, preventing optimistic performance bias.

\textbf{Image Resolution}: Images are resized to $512 \times 512$ pixels. This resolution represents a balance between retaining fine clinical details (such as pneumothorax pleural lines) and staying within the memory constraints of our hardware.

\textbf{Batch Size and Hardware}: Models are trained with a batch size of $32$ on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU (48GB VRAM). The training pipeline utilizes PyTorch and PyTorch Lightning.

\textbf{Optimizer and Learning Rate}: The network is optimized using AdamW \cite{loshchilov2019adamw} with a weight decay of $10^{-4}$. The initial learning rate is set to $10^{-4}$ for the multimodal fusion layers and classification head, and a reduced rate of $10^{-5}$ for the image encoder parameters to prevent pre-trained weight destruction. A cosine annealing learning rate scheduler is applied.

\textbf{Epochs and Early Stopping}: Training is run for a maximum of 20 epochs. Early stopping is applied with a patience of 3 validation checks, monitoring the validation Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) to determine the best model checkpoint.

\textbf{Loss Function}: To address the multi-label long-tailed distribution, ASL \cite{ridnik2021asymmetric} is employed. The negative focusing hyperparameter is set to $\gamma_- = 4$ and the positive focusing to $\gamma_+ = 0$, with an asymmetric clipping threshold of $m = 0.05$ to suppress the gradient contributions of easy negative samples.

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics}: Performance is measured using mAP as the primary metric for long-tailed classification, along with mAUROC and macro-averaged F1 score. For each pathology class \(c\), the Average Precision (AP) is computed over the precision-recall curve as:
\begin{equation}
    AP_c = \sum_n (R_n - R_{n-1}) P_n
\end{equation}
where \(P_n\) and \(R_n\) are the precision and recall at the \(n\)-th threshold. The overall performance is evaluated using mAP:
\begin{equation}
    mAP = \frac{1}{C} \sum_{c=1}^{C} AP_c
\end{equation}
To assess classification balance at a fixed operational threshold, the macro-averaged F1 score is computed:
\begin{equation}
    F1_{macro} = \frac{1}{C} \sum_{c=1}^{C} \frac{2P_cR_c}{P_c + R_c}
\end{equation}
where \(P_c\) and \(R_c\) represent the precision and recall for class \(c\) at a default threshold of \(0.5\). Crucially, macro-averaging treats each pathology class \(c\) equally regardless of prevalence, making it far more sensitive to tail-class performance than micro-averaged metrics. This section reports overall performance as well as breakdown averages across head (frequent), medium, and tail (rare) findings.

\subsection{Main Performance of the Proposed Model}
\label{subsec:results_quantitative}

The performance of all models is evaluated using two primary metrics: mAP and mAUROC. Given the severe long-tailed distribution of the 26 pathology classes, the performance breakdown is reported across three frequency-based categories:
\textbf{Head classes} are highly frequent pathologies, e.g., Support Devices, Pleural Effusion, and Cardiomegaly.

\textbf{Medium classes} are moderately frequent findings, e.g., Atelectasis, Pneumothorax, and Edema.

\textbf{Tail classes} represent rare findings, e.g., Hernia, Pneumomediastinum, and Fibrosis.

Table~\ref{tab:ours_main_results} compares the performance of the three model configurations trained and evaluated on the dataset in this thesis.

\begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \caption{Main test-set performance of the implemented models in this thesis.}
    \label{tab:ours_main_results}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \hline
        Model & mAP & AUROC & Head mAP & Tail mAP \\
        \hline
        Image-only baseline & 0.3246 & 0.8123 & 0.5841 & 0.0827 \\
        Multimodal without prior & 0.4098 & 0.8732 & 0.7116 & 0.1586 \\
        Proposed prior-aware multimodal model & 0.4950 & 0.8985 & 0.7428 & 0.2708 \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The quantitative comparisons demonstrate several key insights:
\textbf{Benefit of Multimodal Fusion}: Moving from an image-only model to the multimodal CaMCheX baseline yields a substantial improvement of $+0.0852$ absolute mAP ($0.3246 \rightarrow 0.4098$) and $+0.0609$ absolute AUROC ($0.8123 \rightarrow 0.8732$). This highlights the diagnostic utility of combining radiographs with ED clinical text and patient vital signs.

\textbf{Impact of Prior-Aware Modeling}: Incorporating historical radiographic and textual context leads to another significant performance leap. The proposed prior-aware framework achieves an overall mAP of $0.4950$ (an absolute increase of $+0.0852$ absolute mAP over the standard multimodal baseline) and an overall AUROC of $0.8985$.

\textbf{Addressing Tail Class Performance}: Long-tailed categories (tail classes) show the most dramatic relative improvements under the prior-aware model, with mAP rising from $0.1586 \rightarrow 0.2708$ ($+0.1122$ absolute mAP) and AUROC increasing to $0.9203$. This indicates that historical summaries and temporal comparisons provide critical stabilization signals for diagnosing rare pathologies.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{subsec:results_ablation}

To systematically investigate the contribution of the individual architectural components of the proposed framework, a series of ablation experiments is conducted. All models are trained under identical hyperparameter settings and evaluated on the test set.

The ablation results highlight several critical aspects of the model's architecture:
\textbf{Modality Contributions}: Integrating clinical text and vital signs with chest radiographs provides the largest single gain ($0.8123 \rightarrow 0.8732$ Macro AUROC), proving that diagnostic decisions benefit significantly from non-visual patient context.

\textbf{Value of Historical Prior}: Adding prior study tokens via cross-attention yields a further boost ($0.8732 \rightarrow 0.8985$ Macro AUROC). This confirms that temporal comparison behaves as a valuable anchor, especially when distinguishing acute changes from stable chronic conditions.

\textbf{Impact of Time-Gap Embeddings}: Removing the discretized time-gap embeddings degrades performance to $0.8845$, demonstrating that the model benefits from knowing the temporal distance of a historical study (e.g. a prior from 2 days ago is treated differently than a prior from 3 years ago).

\textbf{Prior Dropout Regularization}: Training without prior dropout (where prior information is randomly dropped during training) reduces test performance to $0.8901$. This suggests that stochastic prior dropout acts as a vital regularizer, preventing the network from developing shortcut dependencies on historical data and forcing the target stream representation to remain robust.

\subsection{Long-Tail and Per-Class Analysis}
\label{subsec:results_longtail}

To understand how the proposed prior-aware model handles the severe class imbalance, this thesis analyzes performance across individual pathologies in the CXR-LT dataset. Long-tailed classification typically suffers because tail classes have very few positive examples, leading to poor generalization.

The findings indicate that the prior-aware multimodal framework achieves the most substantial improvements on tail classes, with their average AUROC increasing to $0.9203$ (compared to $0.7998$ for the image-only model). This behavior can be explained by two clinical reasons:
\textbf{Semantic Anchoring}: Rare findings such as Hernia, Pneumomediastinum, and Calcification of the Aorta have highly specific textual patterns in clinical indications (e.g., ``evaluate for hiatal hernia'' or ``rule out free air''). Fusing these textual cues compensates for the lack of visual examples in the training set.

\textbf{Temporal Baseline comparison}: For stable, chronic conditions that appear in the tail (such as Tortuous Aorta, Scoliosis, or Fibrosis), the model leverages the prior label and image to confirm that the finding has been historically present, reducing false positives and stabilizing predictions.
